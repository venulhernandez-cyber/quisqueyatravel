Santo Domingo: What to Do Before You Head to the Resort

The oldest city in the Americas — and the most overlooked stop on every Dominican Republic itinerary. Here's what actually matters.

Most visitors to the Dominican Republic land at Las Américas Airport (SDQ), jump in a car, and drive straight to Punta Cana or wherever their resort is. I've done it too. But if you have even one day in Santo Domingo, the capital holds things you simply cannot find in any resort — 500-year-old colonial streets, real Dominican food, and a side of the island that feels genuinely, authentically ours.

The Zona Colonial: the Heart of the City

The Historic Colonial City of Santo Domingo is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — the first European settlement in the Americas, dating back to 1496. That sounds like a textbook fact until you're actually walking the same cobblestone streets that Christopher Columbus's son lived on.

The must-sees: Catedral Primada de América (the oldest cathedral in the New World), Alcázar de Colón (the palace built for Diego Columbus — free to enter the grounds, small museum fee inside), and the Malecón at sunset, one of the most beautiful coastal promenades in the Caribbean.

Local tip: The Zona Colonial is completely walkable — end to end takes about 20 minutes on foot. Don't bother with a car for that stretch. Bring water — the midday heat is intense.

Where to Stay in Santo Domingo

The most practical areas for visitors are the Distrito Nacional itself — specifically between the Zona Colonial, Gazcue, and Piantini. If you want modern hotels near shopping malls and upscale restaurants, Piantini and Naco are the neighborhoods where the money moves.

Options range from boutique colonial hotels at $60–80/night to larger Malecón properties with pools and ocean views.

Where to Eat (Without Paying Tourist Prices)

This is what the Dominican diaspora misses most when they're back home. Real Dominican food — sancocho, chivo al limón, arroz con pollo cooked properly — doesn't exist in resort buffets. It exists in Santo Domingo's comedores.

El Mesón de Bari in the Zona Colonial is a classic stop for local cooking without inflated prices. For breakfast, any comedor in the Gazcue neighborhood will serve you mangú con los tres golpes for under $5 USD. If you want something more elevated, the restaurants along Calle El Conde range from local to international.

Getting Around Santo Domingo

Santo Domingo has traffic — let's be honest about that. For getting around the city, Uber works well and is the safest, most price-transparent option. Public minibuses (conchos and guaguas) are very cheap but if you don't know the routes, skip it.

From Las Américas Airport (SDQ) to the city center: about 25–30 minutes by Uber, should run $10–18 USD depending on traffic. Always check the price before getting in.

Best Time to Visit

Santo Domingo is active year-round. December through April is the dry season — more comfortable weather. July and August are when the Dominican diaspora returns in full force — hot, but the city is electric. The energy of the community coming home creates something special that's worth experiencing at least once.

The Honest Take

Santo Domingo isn't perfect — traffic can be maddening, the midday heat is real, and some areas outside the Distrito Nacional aren't for first-timers. But the Zona Colonial, the Malecón, and real Dominican cooking are experiences no one from the Dominican diaspora should keep skipping.

If you've been visiting the DR for years and only ever seen the resort, add one night in the capital on your next trip. I mean it — it'll change how you see the country.
